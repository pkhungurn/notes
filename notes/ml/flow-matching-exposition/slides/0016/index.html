Derivatives

We are all familiar with derivatives, right?

For a real function $f:\Real \ra \Real$, the derivative of $f'$ is another function of signature $\Real \ra \Real$ such that $$ f'(x) = \lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}$$ for all $x \in \Real$.

This can be written in a different way.

Definition. Let $f: \Real \ra \Real$ be scalar function. The derivative of $f$ at $x$ is the real number $a \in \Real$ such that $$\lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{|f(x+h) - (f(x) + ah)|}{|h|} = 0.$$ A function that sends $x$ to the derivative of $f$ at $x$ is called the derivative of $f$ and is denoted by $f'$.

Derivative of a vector function can be defined in a similarly.

Definition. Let $f: \Real^n \ra \Real^m$ be vector function. The derivative of $f$ at $x$ is the $m \times n$ matrix $A$ such that $$\lim_{h \rightarrow 0} \frac{\|f(x+h) - (f(x) + Ah)\|}{\|h\|} = 0.$$ A function that sends $x$ to the derivative of $f$ at $x$ is called the derivative of $f$.

In other words, near $x$, the value of $f$ can be approximated well by $f(x+h) \approx f(x) + Ah$.

If $f$ sends an $n$-dim vector to an $m$-dim vector,
then the derivative of $f$ sends $n$-dim vector to a matrix of shape $m \times n$.

Some times, the derivative of $f$ at $x$ is called the Jacobian matrix of $f$ at $x$.
