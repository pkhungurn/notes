Derivatives of Simple Functions

Let's compute derivatives of functions we have seen so far.

Translation

Derivative of the translation function $\texttt{T}_{\mu}(x)$ is the identity matrix. \begin{align*} \nabla \texttt{T}_{\mu}(x) = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & \cdots & 0 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & \cdots & 1 \end{bmatrix} = I. \end{align*}

For the 2D case, we have that \begin{align*} \texttt{T}_{\mu}(x) = \begin{bmatrix} x^1 + \mu^1 \\ x^2 + \mu^2 \end{bmatrix}. \end{align*} So, \begin{align*} \nabla T_{\mu}(x) = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{\partial (x^1 + \mu^1)}{\partial x^1} & \frac{\partial (x^1 + \mu^1)}{\partial x^2} \\ \frac{\partial (x^2 + \mu^2)}{\partial x^1} & \frac{\partial (x^2 + \mu^2)}{\partial x^2} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}. \end{align*}

Uniform Scaling

The derivative of a uniform scaling $\texttt{S}_{\sigma}$ is the identity matrix multiplied by a scalar. \begin{align*} \nabla \texttt{S}_{\sigma}(x) = \begin{bmatrix} \sigma & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ 0 & \sigma & \cdots & 0 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & \cdots & \sigma \end{bmatrix} = \sigma I. \end{align*}

For the 2D case, we have that \begin{align*} \texttt{S}_{\sigma}(x) = \begin{bmatrix} \sigma x^1 \\ \sigma x^2 \end{bmatrix}. \end{align*} So, \begin{align*} \nabla S_{\sigma}(x) = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{\partial (\sigma x^1)}{\partial x^1} & \frac{\partial (\sigma x^1)}{\partial x^2} \\ \frac{\partial (\sigma x^2)}{\partial x^1} & \frac{\partial (\sigma x^2)}{\partial x^2} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \sigma & 0 \\ 0 & \sigma \end{bmatrix} = \sigma I. \end{align*}

Scale-Add

The derivative of the scale-add is the same as the derivative of the uniform scaling part of it. $$ \nabla(\texttt{T}_\mu \circ \texttt{S}_\sigma)(x) = \nabla(\sigma\, \mrm{id} + \mu)(x) = \sigma I. $$

Derivation is left as an exercise for the reader.
