Notation for Derivatives of Flows

Derivatives can be quite confusing.

A flow $\phi$ is actually a function of signature $\Real^{d+1} \ra \Real^d$.
However, if you fix $t$, then $\phi_t$ can also be viewed as a function of signature $\Real^d \ra \Real^d$.

So, when we write $\nabla \phi_t(x)$, what do we actually mean?

  1. Do we mean the derivative of $\phi: \Real^{d+1} \ra \Real^d$ applied to $(t,x)$,
    which will give you a matrix of size $d \times (d+1)$?
  2. Do we mean the derivative of $\phi_t: \Real^{d} \ra \Real^d$ applied to $x$,
    which will give you a matrix of size $d \times d$?

As a rule of thumb, when we use $\nabla$ without subscripts,we mean the derivative of function of all arguments. This means Choice 1. As a result, $$\nabla \phi_t(x) \in \Real^{d \times (d+1)}.$$

Choice 2 is actually a partial derivative. It is the derivative with respect to $x$ only. So, we should use Leibniz's notation or a subscript to indicate this. $$ \frac{\partial}{\partial x} \phi_t(x) = \nabla_x \phi_t(x) \in \Real^{d \times d} $$

My notation use indices to indicate which parameter to differentiate with respect to. Since $x$ occupies Dimension $2$ to Dimension $d+1$ of the input vector $(t,x)$, we may write $$ \nabla_{2 \dotsm (d+1)} \phi_t(x)$$ to mean the same thing. This is getting a little unwieldy. I actually write $$ \nabla_{\S 2} \phi_t(x)$$ instead. Here, $\S 2$ indicates the "2nd block" of the input vector.

With the above convention, we can refer to the partial derivative with respect to time with the following notations. $$ \frac{\partial}{\partial t} \phi_t(x) = \nabla_t \phi_t(x) = \nabla_1 \phi_t(x) \in \Real^{d \times 1}.$$

While the partial derivative with respect to time is actually a matrix, we can interpret is as a vector in $\Real^d$.
