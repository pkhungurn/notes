Integrating a Time-Dependent Vector Field

Definition. Let $v$ and $\phi$ be two time-dependent vector fields. We say that $v$ generates $\phi$ if $\phi$ is the solution of the following differential equation \begin{align*} \frac{\partial}{\partial t} \phi_t(x) = v_t(\phi_t(x)) \end{align*} with the initial condition $\phi_0(x) = x$ for all $x$.

The differential equation above can be written as: \begin{align*} \nabla_1 \phi_t(x) = v_t(\phi_t(x)) \end{align*} or \begin{align*} \nabla_1 \phi(t,x) = v(t, \phi(t,x)). \end{align*}

OK. So what does this actually mean?

You notice that, if $v$ generates $\phi$, then $\phi$ is kind of like a flow.

  • $\phi_0(x) = x$.
  • $\phi$ is differentiable with respect to time.

We can interpret $\phi_t(x)$ as the position of the particle that starts at $x$ at time $0$ ends up at time $t$. The differential equation \begin{align*} \frac{\partial}{\partial t} \phi_t(x) = v_t(\phi_t(x)) \end{align*} reads "the rate of change with respect to time of position of the particle that starts at $x$ is given by the vector field." Now,

rate of change of position with respect to time = velociy.

So, what we actually do is physics simulation.

  • At the start, we place a particle at a point $x$.
  • Then, we simulate how the particle moves through space with the respect to the constraint that the velocity of the particle is given by the vector field. So, $v$ acts like a velocity field.
  • $\phi_t(x)$ is the position at time $t$ of the particle that was at $x$ at time $t = 0$.

Let's derive the "flow." We start with \begin{align*} \frac{\partial}{\partial t} \phi_t(x) = \begin{bmatrix} \cos t \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}. \end{align*} So, \begin{align*} \phi_t(x) = x + \int_0^t \begin{bmatrix} \cos u \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}\, \dee u = x + \begin{bmatrix} \sin t \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}. \end{align*} The movement of a particle that starts at $x$ is a simple harmonic motion in the $x^1$-axis around $x$.
