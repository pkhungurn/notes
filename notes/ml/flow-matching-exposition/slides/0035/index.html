Push-Forward

How do we transform one probability distribution to another?

Here's a simple process. Suppose we start with a probability distribution $p$.

  1. Sample $x \sim p$.
  2. Perform some transformation on $x$.
  3. Return the transformed $x$ to the user.

Definition. Let $p: \Real^d \ra \Real^+ \cup \{ 0\}$ be a probability distribution and $f:\Real^d \ra \Real^d$ be a diffeomorphism. A push-forward (or a change of variable) of $p$ according to $f$ is the probability distribution that is created by the following process.

  1. Sample $x \sim p$.
  2. Compute $y = f(x)$.
  3. Return $y$ to the user.

The resulting probability distribution is denoted by $[f]_* p$.

Theorem (Change-of-Variable Formula). Let $q = [f]_*p$. Then, $$ q(y) = \frac{p(f^{-1}(y))}{|\det \nabla f(f^{-1}(y)) |}. $$

The change-of-variable formula above guarantees that $q$ is a probability density function. In other words, \begin{align*} \int q(y)\, \dee y = \int \frac{p(f^{-1}(y))}{|\det \nabla f( f^{-1}(y)) |}\, \dee y = \int p(x)\, \dee x = 1. \end{align*} This agrees with the change of variable formula in multi-variable integral calculus.
